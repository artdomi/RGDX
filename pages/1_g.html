What are electromagnets and how do I control them?

What is an electromagnet?

Let's start with a simpler question: What is a magnet? We've all seen horseshoe and bar magnets before:

They're made out of metal (not just any metal, ferromagnetic metals), and have two poles, north and south. Magnets create invisible lines in space that go from one pole to the other called Magnetic Fields (noted with the letter B).

If you're a piece of iron (or another of these special types of metals), and are placed close to a strong magnet, you will be attracted to the closest pole and will follow the field line to it.

As you may also know, when you get two magnets close, the opposite poles attract, while equal poles repel.

The names North and South are no coincidence. The earth (and most stars and planets) behave like a bar magnet, creating magnetic fields that extend far from the surface. But the bar is upside down?! Why?

We call it the north pole because the north of the compass points there. But a compass is just a magnet that is free to move around and align itself with the earth's field. So (as seen above) the compass north aligns with the earth's magnetic south pole!

In reality, the sun actually pushes the earth's magnetic field away and lead to very interesting phenomena called magnetic reconnection...but let's get back to magnets.

It turns out that electricity, the same one coming out of a wall socket or a battery, can also create magnetic fields. Before we go on, make sure you've read the "What is the electrode voltage and how do I control it?" page, where we discuss the concept of voltages and currents.

In the 19th century, it was discovered that when a current flows (like down a wire), a magnetic field is formed around the current of the same type as the one made by the magnets discussed earlier.

How do I control the electromagnets?

The strength of the field is proportional to the current flowing in the coil, which, in turn, is proportional to the voltage supplied to the coils.

To control the strength of the magnetic field, you can use the red slider. The slider changes the voltage across the coils, changing the current supplied to the magnets leading to a magnetic field at the center of the coils of about 0-130 Gauss (about 300 times the earth's magnetic field).
