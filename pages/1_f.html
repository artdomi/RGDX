What is the electrode voltage and how do I control it?

What's voltage?

You've probably heard the word voltage when talking about batteries and appliances. To start, we should talk about charges:

As you saw in "What is plasma?", all of matter is made out of atoms which are composed of a nucleus and electrons. The nucleus has POSITVE protons and neutral neutrons. Around the nucleus, are the NEGATIVE electrons. We also saw that an ion is an atom that is charged either positively or negatively by changing the number of electrons.

When a conductor (like a metal) has a buildup of charges, be it positive or negative, nature wants to neutralize this by either flowing the excess charges away from it or bringing opposite charges into it.

In the figure, a metal ball has an excess of electrons, therefore it is charged negatively. It wants to discharge either by ejecting electrons (left) or by attracting positive ions (right).

When we have two conducting objects with different charge imbalance, for example, one with excess electrons and the other with excess positive ions (as shown below), then it is said that there is a voltage between the two objects:

The metal objects are called the electrodes.
The flow of charged particles is what we call a current and its symbol is typically I. Note that the current is DEFINED as going in the same direction as the flow if the charges are positive and in the opposite direction if the charges are negative, for example, in the picture below, both the electrons and the ions are creating a current flowing to the right:

So, when there is a voltage between two electrodes and the charged particles are free to move between the electrodes, a current is generated from the more POSITVE electrode to the more NEGATIVE one regardless of what particles are moving. In practice it's usually only the electrons that move.

So, how can we create a voltage in a lab?

In the lab there are voltage differences across battery terminals, power supplies or between electrical socket plug holes.

A battery uses electrochemical cells to keep positive and negative charges separated in the two sides of the battery (or electrodes). By connecting the two electrodes using an electric conductor (a wire for example) you let the charges flow to neutralize them, in other words, we let a current flow.

In a conductor, it is typically the electrons that are mobile, therefore, the current is due to their movement.

Just as a baseball has kinetic energy as it moves, the electrons have energy as they flow. We can use this energy to light up a bulb, for example...or to light up a plasma glow discharge!

Voltage is measured in volts (not surprisingly). A typical AA battery has a voltage of about 2.5 volts, or 2.5V, between the two electrodes. The rectangular ones have 9V.
The wall socket gives a voltage of 120V but it swings positive to negative and back 60 times a second.

In the RGDX, we use a power supply which we can set the voltage manually (or, in your case, remotely). By using the blue voltage control slider, you can set the voltage yourself from 0V to 2000V (almost 20 times higher voltage than the wall socket)!
